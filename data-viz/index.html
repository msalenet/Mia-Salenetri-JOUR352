After the Data Breach

By Mia Salenetri


If you stayed at a hotel owned by Marriott between 2014 and 2018, there’s a chance that hackers have access to some of your personal information. The international hotel chain announced in November that for those four years, more than 500 million customers’ names, addresses, credit card numbers, phone numbers and passport numbers were accessible to hackers. The number was later reduced.


An increase in the number of data breaches and the number of people affected

After an investigation, CEO Arne Sorenson announced the details: up to 383 million guest records and were breached. That involved 18.5 million encrypted passport numbers, 5.25 million unencrypted passport numbers, 9.1 million encrypted payment card numbers, of which 385,000 were valid card numbers. They have not released any further details.



Who's Behind It?



Hackers usually look for specific inconsistencies in security software that they can take advantage of. According to Verizon’s data breach investigation, organized crime groups were behind 39% of breaches, down from almost 80% in 2015. The next most common perpetrators are state-affiliated actors followed by system administrators.





Once they have access to your data, hackers have a few options of where to go next. Usually, they’re able to sell people’s personal data and information on the black market. People buy this information for all kinds of reasons, but the one that most people fear is identity theft.



Identity theft can happen in a couple different forms. Hackers can access your current bank accounts and withdraw money or charge your credit cards. They can sometimes create a totally new credit card account under your name. There’s also tax identity theft and medical identity theft to be aware of. All of these vary in how easy they are for you to pick up on, but the best ways to stay vigilant are to stay up to date on your financial records and check your credit score when you can.



But even when you’re personally protected, what keeps these big companies from getting hacked? And who is held accountable when they do?




You probably remember the 2013 Yahoo data breach, believed to be the biggest in history. Three billion accounts were potentially compromised. As of last month, Yahoo is offering a $117.5 million settlement to the victims who filed a class action lawsuit against the company.


Yahoo, now owned by Verizon, originally proposed a $50 million settlement and offered two years of free credit monitoring for 22 million customers. In January, U.S. District Court Judge Lucy Koh rejected their offer, saying the settlement was not “fundamentally fair, adequate and reasonable.”


Consumers don't just need compensation, they need security.


The response to a data breach is often one of hindsight. Every year we store more and more of our personal information in the cloud. It's more essential than ever that the companies we put our trust in are actively trying to keep our data safe.


“You don’t want to wait until after something has gone wrong to make sure you’re protecting the data to the best of your availability."" Jessica Vitak is an assistant professor in the College of Information Studies at the University of Maryland.

"“As of now, the current approach is consumers are bearing the risk when they decide to create an account, to submit credit card information, or any other sensitive information to a website...They [companies] should be constantly re-evaluating what they currently have in place — are there areas of weakness that make them more vulnerable to being hacked?"


Security is work. And when those companies don't put in the work, we're the ones that suffer the consequences.
